Notes on the Never-Ending Snail Breeding Project

This reflection examines the first six years of a lifelong practice caring for and breeding common garden snails, Cornu aspersum. Given this project's inchoate status, any written account is necessarily provisional. Accordingly, this text moves between factual summary and diaristic contemplation while embracing detours from the project’s stated intent along the way. The easiest title, Snail Breeding Project, is replaced by Never-Ending Snail Breeding Project for this text but there is no fixed title. I’m optimistic that another title will come.

Precursor

Prior to the introduction of the snails were two relevant interactions with exotic pets, which I will describe here. In late 2018, during my final year of college, I purchased a Giant Vietnamese centipede, Scolopendra subspinipes, through an online retailer. The animal was never named and its presence in my studio felt physically threatening bordering on regrettable. I can’t stress enough the speed it possessed–I worried it might escape during a feeding, disappearing in the pipes until a studiomate received an excruciating and potentially lethal bite. I wore thick leather construction gloves and used stainless-steel aquarium tweezers to manipulate the fluffy coconut-husk substrate the centipede would burrow into.

On two occasions, the centipede was fed under questionable circumstances. Twice I sourced green anoles from a pet supply store in Elyria. The manager had me sign a document each time agreeing that the anoles would not be used to feed another animal. Within hours of each feeding, the centipede would leave a perfectly intact anole skeleton. One of these skeletons was mounted on a painting with cyanoacrylate glue. On one occasion, the centipede itself was featured in a student exhibition in front of a tree I had cut down, placed in a clear lidless paint can on a stool for observation by attendees. The maintenance and care of the centipede began to eclipse any other purpose I had for it in a way I did not yet fully understand. Much of this period consisted in observation. At times it felt like staring into a black hole. On a winter trip back home to Los Angeles, the centipede died on the plane, marking an abrupt end to this particular exploration.

After this period with the centipede, I moved back in with my parents and prepared to buy a hatchling green anaconda, Eunectes murinus, from an anaconda breeder I had been following online named Meghan Kelley. Meghan came to my attention via a breeding project I was particularly interested in at the time, the Reach Out Reptiles super dwarf reticulated python project, which seeked to create a chihuahua-like, miniature version of the world’s longest snake species. This breeder had collected a few of her anacondas. I put down the deposit and purchased necessary starting materials: heat pads, a bespoke drawer-like snake enclosure, lighting, troughs. There was an exotic pet exposition in Anaheim, CA where we planned the pickup. My priority in owning the snake was the creation of a sculpture using a non-functional but water-filled toilet with a complete female green anaconda defecation inside of it. This would be installed near the far wall of a space, with the other walls hosting mounted live baby snake containers of various morphs paired with customized Starbucks drinks. An example pairing: tall Pumpkin Cream Cold Brew with no ice, extra foam, and an extra shot of espresso with a baby rainbow boa, Epicrates cenchria, expressing an incomplete dominant Picasso Stripe gene. The sex specificity of the green anaconda put more pressure on things, as the females are significantly larger than the males. The ten year logistical nightmare of owning the giant snake was supposed to ‘figure itself out.’ On the freeway to Anaheim to pick up my selected anaconda, I texted the breeder that I was no longer interested, giving up my deposit and shelving the project.

Snails

Abandoning the anaconda coincided with the unplanned acquisition of the basal population of Never-Ending Snail Breeding Project garden snails. I removed nine snails from a friend’s backyard in Encino, which his parents had just begun to treat with poison. The decision was made after an extended period spent with them in one large planter, flipping over leaves revealing tens of snails of different sizes and stages of their life cycle, incredibly small, small, medium, extra large. A snail is the kind of animal you see just sparingly enough for it to deliver surprise when given a second glance–on this night in particular I was very overtaken by my impression of the garden snail as an animal species. They attracted infinite gazing. I was trying to move away from milking artworks from these animals I was buying and focus more on their care and continued existence in my life, a struggle that has remained unresolved. Not realizing what was to come, I took their scheduled demise as a sign and left his house that night with the snails in tow. The project began.

A few things about the snails captivated me. They move faster than their proverbial pace suggests–remarkably fast for their mode of locomotion. These snails’ shells were almost indistinguishable prima facie–I could pick out the darkest and lightest shells, maybe a shell that had been cracked and healed, but that was it. My first impulse was to train my eye to notice more subtle differences between snail shells, but in subsequent years the shells have had unexpected emergent properties. These will be touched on shortly.

Snail digestion has outlasted some of these other qualities as a primary fascination. Snails and other gastropods consume their food with an organ called a radula, covered in tooth-like structures that scrape off flecks of food and move them to the esophagus. While their method of digestion resembles that of most animals, snails uniquely produce cellulases to break down plant matter. Though still a subject of debate, snails seem to produce these enzymes themselves rather than employ gut flora, as snail guts mostly contain microorganisms already present in the soil, if any. I was taken by this detail, and inspection of the snail defecation showed something else unique. Vegetal matter is minimally altered–scraped flecks are held together in transparent mucus, highlighting the original pigmentation of the food. Their defecation resembled paint out of the tube.

It was clear in the first few days that these snails were the point of convergence for several interests and strategies I was exploring during this period. I sensed this would be a life-long engagement, and that this resembled the point. Bonsai had grown as one of these parallel interests. Yamadori in particular, which involves the pulling of a naturally crippled tree from a mountainside or similar terrain to use as a bonsai starter, felt nearly identical to the project I had set out on. Bonsai regularly outlive their owners, who pass their trees down for generations. I tried and failed to establish a yamadori sugar pine during this time. The snail project was in part modeled after this tradition: maintaining an unbroken generational lineage of snails to be cared for indefinitely.

As a result of the project’s immense scale, I’ve time and time again contradicted some of my most basic feelings about how the Never-Ending Snail Breeding Project is related to art, on occasion felt that it had nothing to do with art, and with some frequency wondered what exactly I was doing. In 2020, TikTok became a central venue for publicly sharing the snails as snail defecation became a primary material produced in overwhelming volume by the project. The action of applying this substance to canvases, paper, and sculpture using my fingers was recorded in 15-30 second videos. A few of these videos still exist. As previously mentioned, the colorful defecation mystified me. Since the beginning, the snails have had a primary diet of dark leafy greens like collards, kale, and carrots along with the usual supplement of cuttlefish “bone”. These foods produce a deep green and radiant orange in the defecation. The orange pigment in particular fades within days of drying out. No effort was made to prevent the deterioration of these pigments. Both direct artistic practice and indirect promotion, this profile building on TikTok and other platforms may have briefly contributed to the project's visibility, though the people paying attention had no context to understand that this was a project in the first place. Concurrently, a practice of naming and photographing individual snails emerged as part of a broader attempt to recognize and document subtle variations, including an archive of shell measurements taken with a caliper. This practice was eventually dropped along with digital and physical attempts at rendering portraits to record the detail of my observation. The identification of unique traits amongst the snails has waned almost completely from the project by now, but will reenter moving forward. Pairings of orange and green persist as a motif of the project, along with use of snail defecation.

Snails reproduce frequently, laying between 40-80 eggs beneath the soil. Allowing this cycle to go on unchecked would plummet the quality of life of any snail owner’s project. As a result, most snail hobbyists are forced to cull, or selectively slaughter, the snail offspring. This is usually an opportunity to select certain traits and reject others, such as color, size, and general capacity to stay alive. Due to the unusual circumstances of the Never-Ending Snail Breeding Project, I have never been sure what exactly to actively select for. Slaughtering countless baby snails is my reality, and I usually achieve this by throwing them out my window. My tendency is to let more than I should survive, that is if I’ve let the snails hatch. At times there are multiple egg clutches visible beneath the soil, which gives me an opportunity to randomly select one to destroy. On multiple occasions I’ve tasted this snail caviar, milky pearlescent eggs that, once cleaned of any dirt, taste earthy and rich. Some friends have suggested snail farming specifically for caviar should be one of the aims of the project, but I don’t have the space or really the desire to do so.

Further layers of meaning accumulated as I realized the snails were storing physical records of their care and neglect within their shells. Analogous to geological strata or ice core samples, each shell offers a material archive containing potentially interpretable information about the state of contemporary art practice at large and the personal habits of myself as their caretaker. These habits have swung in the direction of neglect for extended periods. In 2021, while on an international tour as part of the Whistlegraph artist group, the snails survived in my studio for a month without care. But the evidence of my neglect was obvious and permanent for this population, with almost every still-developing snail exhibiting at least one growth line of neglect. Many snails have died, and I’ve taken care to preserve their shells. This has felt less important more recently–some of these shells have been used as painting supports in and of themselves, painted to resemble simple chameleon heads or in a monochrome brown. I’m confused by these snail-induced artworks, and feel that acknowledging them here may give them unwarranted attention. But this is a marathon, not a sprint, and these are the realities of the project.

A brief image that explains my mindset during this time, roughly 2020-2022. My primary focus was the daily practice with my collaborators in Whistlegraph, which pulled attention away from the snails. As a result of this new responsibility, the snails frequently took the backseat. A reoccuring dream during this time involved encountering vast towers of terrariums, dirty and stinking, abandoned for what seemed like years in awful, dark rooms. To my horror, as I’d peer into these terrariums, the small animals inside were somehow still, just barely, alive.

Alien Hybrid Monsters

Within the first year with the snails, the project projected a far horizon of biological speculation. I am nurturing the potential for genetic transformation through selective breeding and neglectful care, with the goal of hybridizing these snails with other mollusk species, particularly cephalopods like cuttlefish, aiming to produce a new, intelligent mosaic life form capable of novel forms of interspecies communication. Additions of other genetic materials, extraterrestrial or not, are welcome depending on what discoveries are made over the next million years. This is the farthest reaching vision for what this project could realize. Initially framed as an “ambient mutation tank”, casually irradiating the snails with ionizing waves from bananas and cosmic rays, I’ve doubled down on the project's mutative aims. During two public presentations of the snails at the San Francisco Art Book Fair and Decentralized Web Camp, the slow mutation of the lineage in my studio felt sufficiently teased out. But after these lectures, the hybridizing and creation of a novel animal became the focus of all changes within the genome I was nurturing. Around this period, the project resembled an unfundable analogue artificial intelligence startup with only a vague utility in mind.

This hybrid monster would exhibit a unique intelligence enabling communication in a novel way when measured against all current human to non-human communications. The evolutionary gap between vertebrates and invertebrates would be bridged through these charismatic new invertebrate representatives. I do believe in the creative transformation of life itself, not towards a Caligarian anthropomorphization of everything but towards a fundamental desire for an increase in difference and complexity. Not in the creation of, to use a contemporary example, a hyper-productive AI limited by our knowledge until it naturally escapes that, but a creation with that escape as the aim. An actively collaborative, mutualistic symbiosis. I may be too attached to the monstrous description, as I don’t imagine nor desire this species to be a disharmonic chimera.I prefer not to imprison this creature within the limits of my imagination. I’m trying to respect morphological designs I could never fully grasp in their emergence–to allow them to express themselves. When I use the term monster, I am borrowing that term to describe the products of a truly creative and courageous play with the materials of life itself. If the snails are to be this path towards creating a new form of life through contemporary methods, they will likely require the efforts and funds of multiple parties. But making the poetic case comes first.

Conclusion

My lifetime with the snails will not coincide with the artwork the project seeks to realize. Even if that artwork is the alien hybrid monster, only over magaanums, if this lineage of snails remains unbroken through speciation or hybridization, would a point or points along this population’s evolutionary gradient become self-evident as an artwork. By this far future point, even within 10,000 years, “art” may be an extinct term. Our own species will have transformed into something else. It becomes unimaginable, beyond speculation. But the most crucial qualities of art will surely persist, its extra-dimensional qualities perceptible by an audience we don’t have words to describe. Whatever it is that replaces art might ring at the same frequency we’ve come to associate with what’s best about art. I must stress that this recurring vision is as susceptible to transformation as the genetic information of the snails themselves during the life of this project. Had I written this a year or two ago, I would have insisted I had no clear agenda in sight. This project will exist for so long that it will outlive the many things I’ve thought about it over the years, including what I may think is its critical purchase in this text. Choosing a goal is as easy as writing it down, but more than anything just keeping these snails alive is what concerns me most. Another goal will likely reveal itself in time.

I’m in my sixth year of breeding these snails over what is now surely the 13th 14th 15th 16th(?) generation. Their rapid reproduction means a few months of relative detachment from the project can confuse generation counting. There are still times I walk into my studio and expect them all to be dead. I am a terrible snail owner. There are periods of boredom with the project followed by focused observation and maintenance. The snails have moved between California, Oregon, and New York. When preparing to move them to New York, I decreased the population by tossing many of the snails in gardens and yards in my Los Angeles neighborhood as a sort of insurance in the event of a total die-off. I’d like to think that any snail I find in those yards contains some genetic continuity with the original lineage.

Improvements can be made. By this point the rampant incest must be curtailed. I’m considering the introduction of one or two individuals from outside the project to increase genetic diversity. Simultaneously, forking off specifically selected couples in reproduction tanks would begin the process of selecting for specific traits. These traits need only to be determined for reasons somewhat arbitrary (shell color, shape, and pattern) or better suited for survival in the project (shell thickness, snail size). I’d like to provide the snails with more space in the coming years. Close quarters can decrease snail activity due to constant interaction with one another’s mucus trails. The plan is to design a circular dish about the size of a washing machine window or larger in which a mound of clay-abundant, sand-heavy soil and rock can be sculpted, with a breeding mote of softer substrate around the perimeter. A large acrylic cloche with a handle can be used to cover this mound.

Ultimately, while extensive textual reflections and conceptual tangents remain secondary to the central breeding and care practice, they serve an essential role in maintaining the project's visibility and interest over potentially vast timescales. These written records may prove crucial in securing future engagement and funding, ensuring the project's continuity and its potential transformation across generations. There is a possibility of this.
