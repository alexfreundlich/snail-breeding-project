Notes on the Never‑Ending Snail Breeding Project

Updated · Jun 25 2025

This reflection examines the first six years of a lifelong practice caring for and breeding common garden snails, Cornu aspersum. Given the project’s inchoate status, any written account is necessarily provisional. Accordingly, this text moves between factual summary and diaristic contemplation while embracing detours from the project’s stated intent along the way. The easiest title, Snail Breeding Project, is replaced by Never‑Ending Snail Breeding Project for this text but there is no fixed title. I’m optimistic that another title will come.

Precursor

Prior to the introduction of the snails were two relevant interactions with exotic pets, which I will describe here. In late 2018, during my final year of college, I purchased a Giant Vietnamese centipede, Scolopendra subspinipes, through an online retailer. The animal was never named and its presence in my studio felt physically threatening bordering on regrettable. I can’t stress enough the speed it possessed–I worried it might escape during a feeding, disappearing in the pipes until a studiomate received an excruciating and potentially lethal bite. I wore thick leather construction gloves and used stainless-steel aquarium tweezers to manipulate the fluffy coconut-husk substrate the centipede would burrow into.

On two occasions the centipede was fed under questionable circumstances. I sourced green anoles from a pet supply store in Elyria. The manager had me sign a document agreeing that the anoles would not be used to feed another animal. Within hours of feeding, the centipede would leave a perfectly intact anole skeleton. On one occasion, the centipede was featured in a student exhibition, placed in a clear paint can on a stool for observation by attendees. The maintenance and care of the centipede began to eclipse any ephemera related to it in a way I did not yet fully understand. Much of this period consisted in observation. At times it felt like staring into a black hole–presymbolic information whizzing past my skull. On a winter trip back home to Los Angeles, the centipede died on the plane, marking an abrupt end to this particular exploration.

After the centipede I moved back in with my parents and prepared to buy a hatchling green anaconda, Eunectes murinus, from an anaconda breeder I had been following online named Meghan Kelley. I put down the deposit and purchased necessary starting materials. There was an exotic pet exposition in Anaheim, CA where we planned the pickup. My priority in owning the snake was creating a sculpture consisting of a non-functional toilet filled with water and a complete female green anaconda defecation inside of it. This would be installed near the far wall of a space, with the other walls hosting mounted live baby snake containers of various morphs paired with customized Starbucks drinks. An example pairing: tall Pumpkin Cream Cold Brew with no ice, extra foam, and an extra shot of espresso with a rainbow boa, Epicrates cenchria, expressing an incomplete dominant Picasso Stripe gene. The sex specificity of the green anaconda put more pressure on things, as the females are significantly larger than the males. The ten year logistical nightmare of owning the giant snake was supposed to ‘figure itself out.’ On the freeway to Anaheim to pick up my selected anaconda, I texted the breeder that I was no longer interested, giving up my deposit and shelving the project.

Snails

Abandoning the anaconda coincided with the unexpected acquisition of garden snails. Nine snails were removed from a friend’s backyard in Encino, which his parents had just begun to treat with poison. The decision was made after an extended period spent with them in one large planter, flipping over leaves revealing tens of snails of different sizes and stages of their life cycle, incredibly small, small, medium, extra large. The whole encounter had this feeling of reunion–a snail is the kind of animal you see just sparingly enough for it to deliver surprise when given a second glance–and on this night in particular I was very overtaken by my impression of the garden snail as an animal species. Leaving his house that night with the snails in tow, the project began.

A few things about the snails captivated me. They move faster than their reputation suggests–remarkably fast for their mode of locomotion. These snails’ shells were almost indistinguishable prima facie–I could pick out the darkest and lightest shells, maybe a shell that had been cracked and healed, but that was it. My first impulse was to train my eye to notice more subtle differences between snail shells (the shells have had more to offer as the project progresses). Snail digestion has outlasted the others as a primary fascination. Snails and other gastropods consume their food with an organ called a radula, covered in tooth-like structures that scrape off flecks of food and move them to the esophagus. While their method of digestion resembles that of most animals, snails uniquely produce cellulases to break down plant matter. Though still a subject of debate, snails seem to produce these enzymes themselves rather than employ gut flora, as snail guts mostly contain microorganisms already present in the soil, if any. I was taken by this detail, and inspection of the snail defecation showed something else unique. Vegetal matter is minimally altered–scraped flecks are held together in a tube of mucus highlighting the original pigmentation of the food. Their defecation resembled paint out of the tube.

It was clear in the first few days that these snails were the point of convergence for several interests and strategies that had been overtaking me during this period. I sensed this would be a life-long engagement. Bonsai had grown as one of these parallel interests. Yamadori in particular, which involves the pulling of a naturally crippled tree from a mountainside or similar terrain to use as a bonsai starter, felt uncannily similar to the project I had set out on. Bonsai regularly outlive their owners, who pass their trees down for generations. The snail project was in part modeled after this tradition: maintaining an unbroken generational lineage of snails to be cared for indefinitely.

As a result of the project’s immense scale, experiments and ephemera have come and gone to help pass the time despite possibly contradicting some of my most basic feelings about how the snail breeding project is related to art. In 2020, TikTok became a venue for publicly sharing the snails as snail defecation became a primary material produced in overwhelming volume by the project. The action of applying this substance to canvases, paper, and sculptures using my fingers was recorded in 15-30 second videos. A few of these videos still exist. As previously mentioned, the colorful defecation mystified me. Since the beginning, the snails have had a primary diet of dark leafy greens like collards and kale, and carrots along with the usual supplement of cuttlefish “bone”. These foods produce a deep green and radiant orange in the defecation. The orange pigment in particular fades within days of drying out. No effort was made to seal these snail defecation works. Both direct artistic practice and indirect promotion, this profile work briefly contributed to the project's visibility. Concurrently, a practice of naming and photographing individual snails emerged as part of a broader attempt to recognize and document subtle variations, but was eventually dropped along with digital and physical attempts at rendering portraits to record the detail of my observation. Pairings of orange and green persist as a motif of the project, along with use of snail defecation.

Further layers of meaning accumulated as the snails began storing physical records of their care and neglect within their shells. Analogous to geological strata or ice core samples, these shells offered material archives potentially interpretable as reflections of contemporary art practice and the personal habits of myself as an artist. And these habits swung in the direction of neglect for extended periods. In 2021, while on a month-long international tour as part of the Whistlegraph artist group, the snails survived in my studio without care. But the evidence of my neglect was obvious and permanent, with almost every snail exhibiting at least one line of neglect early on in their shell formation. This was a marathon, not a sprint.

I’ll briefly mention my mindset during this time, roughly 2020-2022. My primary focus was the daily practice with my friends in Whistlegraph, which pulled attention away from the snails. As a result of this new responsibility, the project entered its first extended period of neglect. A reoccuring dream during this time involved encountering vast towers of terrariums, dirty and stinking, abandoned for what seemed like years. To my horror, as I’d peer into these terrariums, the small animals inside were somehow still clinging to life.

Alien Hybrid Monsters

From the outset, the project has projected a far horizon of biological speculation. I am nurturing the potential for genetic transformation through selective breeding and neglectful care, with the goal of hybridizing these snails with other mollusk species, particularly cephalopods, aiming to produce a new, intelligent mosaic life form capable of novel forms of interspecies communication. Initially framed as an ambient mutation tank, casually irradiating the snails with ionizing waves from bananas and cosmic rays, the project's goals have since expanded. Through public presentations at San Francisco Art Book Fair and Decentralized Web Camp, the more monstrous ends of the project were teased out. These presentations emphasized the speculative and utopian potentials rather than practical or ethical limitations, positioning the project as a persistent and provocative suggestion to future generations. Around this period, the project resembled an unfunded analogue artificial intelligence startup.

The snails are a path towards creating a new form of life through contemporary methods likely requiring the efforts and funds of multiple parties. This hybrid monster will exhibit a unique intelligence enabling communication in a novel way when measured against all current human to non-human communications. The evolutionary gap between vertebrates and invertebrates would be bridged through these charismatic new invertebrate representatives. I believe in the creative transformation of life itself, not towards a Caligarian anthropomorphization of everything but towards a fundamental desire for an increase in difference and complexity. Not the creation of a hyper-productive AI limited by our knowledge until it naturally escapes that, but a creation with escape as the aim. The workplace hazard of such an undertaking may in theory be the creation of a bioweapon but I’m sure that won't happen. I believe the time is now to start creating new life forms. My snails are aliens in the making.

Conclusion

My lifetime with the snails will not coincide with the artwork the project seeks to realize. Only over magaanums, if this lineage of snails remains unbroken through speciation or hybridization, then there will be a point along that gradient that would be the artwork. My intent is that a point or points along this population’s evolutionary gradient would become self-evident as an artwork. By this far future point, even within 10,000 years, “art” may be an extinct term. Our own species will have transformed into something else. It becomes unimaginable, beyond speculation. But the most crucial qualities of art will surely persist, its extra-dimensional qualities perceptible by an audience we don’t have words to describe. Whatever it is that replaces art might ring at the same frequency we’ve come to associate with what’s best about art. I must stress that this recurring vision is as susceptible to transformation as the genetic information of the snails themselves during the life of this project. Had I written this a year or two ago, I might have insisted this project had no clear agenda in sight. Choosing a goal is as easy as writing it down, but more than anything just keeping these snails alive is what concerns me most. Another goal may reveal itself in time.

I’m in my sixth year of breeding these snails over what is now surely the 13th 14th 15th 16th(?) generation. They reproduce so rapidly that a few months of detachment from the project can confuse generation counting. There are still times I walk into my studio and expect them all to be dead. I am a terrible snail owner. There are periods of boredom with the project followed by focused observation and maintenance. Ultimately, while extensive textual reflections and conceptual tangents remain secondary to the central breeding and care practice, they serve an essential role in maintaining the project's visibility and interest over potentially vast timescales. These written records may prove crucial in securing future engagement and funding, ensuring the project's continuity and its potential transformation across generations. There is a possibility of this.
